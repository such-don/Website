##  

Dear Ms Cheslow,

<br/>

Now that the semester is nearly over, I would like to take this opportunity to comment on what I believed to be my strengths and goals at the beginning of this course, as well as how those ideas have changed. Quite honestly, when I wrote this memo back then, I had no idea what my strengths were. As a result, I ended up calling some minor common sense I had when it came to notation in technical writing a strength. However, thanks to the feedback of yourself and others, as well as my own self-reflection, I can begin to pinpoint what makes me a good writer (as well as what I need to improve upon). 

I believe that I am able to generate complex ideas that cause the reader to be interested in my writing. I mean this in the sense that my writing causes the reader to stop and ponder. My writing process consists of toiling over each and every sentence before moving on to the next one (which is apparently not what everyone does as I found out in that one class we had). I put a generous amount of thought into each statement that I make, and I believe that this comes across to the reader. A perfect example of this was my literacy narrative. I had several complex points to make in that narrative, and it simply was not possible to understand the gist of what I was saying unless the reader thought about the points themselves and how they applied to their own life. However, the fact that the reader could only get the gist of my points meant that even the revised version lacked the clarity I desired. My goal at the beginning of this semester was to improve the clarity of my writing, and although I have definitely improved, this will continue to be my goal likely for the rest of my life. 

I also believe that I am able to present myself well to others through my writing. Perhaps because I am a little braggadocious in general, but I have never had trouble being able to convey why I am good at doing something. What I mean in particular is the analysis of what the recipient is specifically looking for and tailoring my achievements and qualities to align with them. Quite an obvious example of this is my cover letter. However, as a result, I can't help but feel that this comes across as ingenuine or overconfident. This is an issue that I face constantly in many facets which I hope to improve upon in the future. During my PhD applications, I was constantly toeing the line between "I was put on this earth to do research in area x" and "x is cool but I am interested in y too". If that was confusing, I basically still don't know how to convey genuine interest.

Finally, I believe that I am not able to collaboratively write with others that well. As I head into academia, collaboration with other researchers is inevitable. Like many other students, I am not a fan of group work, especially when my collaborators are selected at random. Either I don't feel as though my partners are not up to standard, or I am the one severely lacking in some department, which has never been the case for me in my life. Of course, this is a pretty subjective and ill-defined dichotomy, but I believe this is precisely why I am bad at group work. Because I have never been someone who needed to catch up I find it hard to put myself in the position of those that do. Despite this, I try to be respectful to everyone in my group, and this sometimes causes me to not just tell the other person that I think they are wrong. A prime example of this was my experience during the collaborative analysis project. I think this prioritization of group morale (or at least that is what I believed I was doing) ended up hurting the group in the long run. That said, being nice and getting a good grade are obviously not mutually exclusive ideas, and as a result, I hope to improve on this area in the future

<br/>

Sincerely,

Suchetan Dontha

## **Reflective Portfolio Summary**

For my portfolio, I decided to choose the culmination of the work of three different projects I have worked on over the past few years. The first is the manuscript that was submitted to a conference for a project on quantum computing that I was a part of. I included this piece because it is by far the most involved project I have been a part of and hence the piece that I am most proud of. It is also the first and only academic paper I have written, and as I'm attempting to become a researcher, this allows me to showcase my technical writing skills and also what I am capable of in the field. A current weakness of this piece is that it is not publicly available since it is not brushed up yet. However, my collaborators and I plan to polish it up and put it on the arxiv in the coming months, so in the future, I will change it to go there instead. The next piece I included is a poster that was made for a project on applying quantum computing to a problem in math. The strength of including this is twofold. Firstly, I can showcase my ability to make a subject presentable and understandable (to those in the field of course). But secondly, it showcases that I am competent in both mathematics and computer science. The final piece I include is a poster that was made for a math project I was on. I included this to show my competency with programming as well as math. However, a weakness of including posters, in general, is that it is not seen as very substantive work - having a technical report is almost always going to be a better representation of ability. That being said, I don't have any other papers I have written, so all I can do is add them to this website as they come.
